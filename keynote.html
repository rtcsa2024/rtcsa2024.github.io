Keynote Speech 1



Driving the Future of Mobility: Real-Time Systems in Software-Defined Vehicles

Speaker: Seongsoo Hong, Seoul National University, Korea

Abstract:

The automotive industry is undergoing a profound transformation with the rise of software-defined vehicles (SDVs). These next-generation vehicles, driven by advanced software technologies, are poised to revolutionize mobility, safety, and user experience and create a new revenue engine for the automotive industry. At the heart of this evolution lies the critical role of real-time computing systems. This keynote will explore the intersection of SDVs and real-time technologies, highlighting the challenges and opportunities that await the real-time community.

First, I will define what an SDV is and present the significant industrial value it creates. Then, I will explain the four background factors that have led to the rapid rise of SDVs in recent times. Through this examination, we will recognize that SDVs are simultaneously cloud-native computing systems and mixed-criticality systems. In turn, I will introduce the standardized centralized zonal hardware architecture and the software platform operating on top of it, which are essential for realizing SDVs with these characteristics. It is important to note that the runtime system of an SDV must simultaneously satisfy the dynamic extendibility of applications and the static assurance of mixed-criticality services, which are somewhat contradictory requirements. The automotive industry has made significant efforts to meet these requirements but has been hindered by various technical challenges.

Over the past 40 years, the real-time computing community has built a profound academic foundation and made numerous innovations. With this knowledge, the real-time computing community has many opportunities to assist the automotive industry in its pursuit of SDV realization and lead the way toward the future of mobility. Based on this recognition, as a member of the real-time computing community, I will propose ways in which we can contribute to the realization of SDVs with a sense of mission. Join us as we navigate the road ahead, where real-time systems play a pivotal role in shaping the future of mobility through software-defined vehicles.

Bio:

Dr. Seongsoo Hong has been a professor in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at Seoul National University for 30 years. He received his Bachelor's and Master's degrees in Computer Engineering from Seoul National University and his Ph.D. in Computer Science from the University of Maryland, USA, with a focus on real-time systems design. Over the past 35 years, he has actively conducted academic research into real-time operating systems, middleware, design methodologies for real-time and embedded systems, with a focus on their application to mobile systems, telecommunication systems, and automotive systems. He is one of the founding members of IEEE RTCSA and a fellow of the National Academy of Engineering of Korea.

Professor Hong's work extends beyond academic research. He has served as a technology advisor to Samsung Advanced Institute of Technology, Samsung Electronics Wireless Division, Samsung Semiconductor, Hyundai Autonet, and Hyundai Transys. He has also been an outside director for companies such as Hanwha Systems, LS Automotive, and Mercury System. As a result of these activities, he successfully industrialized the real-time operating system (RTOS) developed in his laboratory, contributing to the development of Velos, Neos, and SMK used in defense equipment such as KAI's advanced trainer aircraft and Samsung Electronics' product lines.

Professor Hong's recent research focuses on the design of mission-critical systems with real-time capabilities. A prime example is the software-defined vehicle (SDV), which has garnered significant attention as the core of future automobiles in the automotive industry. Over the past five years, Professor Hong has devoted efforts to concretizing the concept of SDV, raising public awareness, and presenting software engineering technologies necessary for SDV to evolve into an industrial reality. Currently, he is researching a MCS architecture framework with probabilistic guarantees, an essential technology in SDVs that ensures functional safety and mixed-criticality.

In addition to his academic and industrial activities, Professor Hong has been continuously engaged in community service. He served as the director of the Future Innovation Program (FIP) at Seoul National University, a continuing education program for entrepreneurs and policymakers. Currently, he is a board member of the Korea Polytechnics, an institution dedicated to nurturing future engineering talents. Furthermore, he has contributed to the development of the Korean Society of Automotive Engineers by establishing the Automotive Semiconductor and System S/W Division, where he serves as the inaugural chairman.

Keynote Speech 2



A Personal Retrospect of 30 some years of Computer Systems Research in Korea

Speaker: Sam H.(Hyuk) Noh, Virginia Tech College of Engineering, USA

Abstract:

In this talk, I will look back into the 30 some years I have spent throughout my academic career while serving as a professor in Korean institutions, and beyond.

Through this retrospect, I draw some life lessons that I have learned and hopefully will give the next generations of young researchers, not just in Korea, but worldwide, to ponder over as they pursue their journey in computer systems research.

While most of the talk will be based on experiential anecdotes, a sprinkle of technical wisdom will be sprinkled here and there as well.

Bio:

Sam H.(Hyuk) Noh received the BE degree in computer engineering from the Seoul National University, Seoul, Korea, in 1986, and the PhD degree from the Department of Computer Science, University of Maryland, College Park, MD, in 1993. He held a visiting faculty position at the George Washington University, Washington, DC, from 1993 to 1994 before joining Hongik University, in Seoul, Korea, where he was a professor in the School of Computer and Information Engineering until the Spring of 2015. Starting from the Fall of 2015 he joined UNIST (Ulsan National Institute of Science and Technology), where he was a Professor at the Department of Computer Science and Engineering and served as the inaugural Dean of the Graduate School of Artificial Intelligence in the College of Information and Biotechnology from August 2020 through March 2023. He also served as the Dean of the School of Electrical and Computer Engineering from January of 2016 through June of 2018. As of January 2023, he is a Professor at the Computer Science Department at Virginia Tech. He has served as General Chair, Program Chair, and Program Committee Member on a number of technical conferences and workshops including USENIX ATC, USENIX FAST, ACM Eurosys, USENIX HotStorage, ACM EMSOFT, ACM SOSP, IEEE RTAS, ACM ASPLOS, USENIX OSDI, ACM LCTES, IEEE ICPADS, and WWW, among others. He is currently the Steering Committee Chair for ACM HotStorage and a Steering Committee member of USENIX FAST and IEEE NVMSA. As of May 2024, he is serving as co-Editor-in-Chief of the ACM Transactions on Computer Systems (TOCS). He also served as Editor-in-Chief of the ACM Transactions on Storage (TOS) from 2016 through 2022. His research interests include system software issues pertaining to computer systems in general and storage systems in particular, with a focus on the use of new memory technologies such as flash memory and persistent memory. He is a Fellow of the ACM and the IEEE and a member of USENIX and KIISE (Korean Institute of Information Scientists and Engineers).

Keynote Speech 3



The Future of Processing Units: From CPU to GPU and Beyond

Speaker: Won Woo Ro, Yonsei University, Korea

Abstract:

In the ever-evolving landscape of computer systems and architecture, processing units stand at the heart of technological innovation. This keynote address, "The Future of Processing Units: From CPU to GPU and Beyond," embarks on a comprehensive journey through the past, present, and future of processing technologies. We will begin by exploring the historical evolution and foundational role of Central Processing Units (CPUs) in driving computational capabilities and system development. Following this, we explore into the rise of Graphics Processing Units (GPUs), highlighting their transformative and broad impact on parallel processing and AI applications.

As we navigate the current state of processing units, we will inspect cutting-edge developments and innovations that are pushing the boundaries of what is possible in computing. This includes an overview of emerging technologies such as NPU(neural processing units) and other specialized accelerators that are redefining performance and efficiency metrics. Additionally, the session will cover emerging paradigms like Processing-In-Memory (PIM) and in-storage computing, which are revolutionizing data processing by bringing computation closer to data, thereby improving performance and energy efficiency.

Looking ahead, we will envision the future of processing units, discussing the potential breakthroughs and challenges that lie on the horizon. Topics will include advancements in quantum computing and quantum processing units (QPU). Attendees will gain insights into how these evolving technologies will shape the future of computing, impacting industries ranging from healthcare and finance to entertainment and beyond. Join us for an insightful session that not only celebrates the milestones of processing units but also inspires innovative thinking about the technological frontiers that await us.

Bio:

Won Woo Ro received the B.S. degree in electrical engineering from Yonsei University, Seoul, South Korea, in 1996, and the M.S. and Ph.D. degrees in electrical engineering from the University of Southern California, in 1999 and 2004, respectively. He worked as a Research Scientist with the Electrical Engineering and Computer Science Department, University of California, Irvine. He currently works as a Professor with the School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, Yonsei University. Prior to joining Yonsei University, he worked as an Assistant Professor with the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering, California State University, Northridge. His industry experience includes a college internship with Apple Computer, Inc., and a contract software engineer with ARM, Inc. His current research interests include high-performance microprocessor design, GPU microarchitectures, neural network accelerators, and memory hierarchy design.
